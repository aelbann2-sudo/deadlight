\section{Project Overview}

Deliverable 2 is a playable Unity prototype of \textit{Deadlight} that is more complete than the initial concept build. The submission covers \textbf{Levels 1 and 2} only, which together form six playable nights across two districts. This scope gives a focused sample of the full game while keeping development realistic for the mid-project milestone.

The core loop has three phases. By day, the player explores, follows objectives, and prepares for the next attack. At night, the player survives zombie waves as pressure increases. After dawn, the player receives points and rewards, then uses the shop to buy weapons, armor, ammunition, healing, and upgrades before the next cycle. This rhythm links exploration, combat, and progression in one experience.

The main design goal was to make \textbf{preparation matter}. Earlier builds risked treating daytime as a passive break between fights. In the current build, daytime choices affect the night through objectives, contested drops, and resources, so players can see how scavenging and spending relate to survival. Daytime favors planning what to secure before night locks in; nighttime favors quick reactions under pressure. Narrative context comes from radio, environmental detail, and missions tied to EVAC Command, Project Lazarus, Dr.~Chen, and Subject~23.

This report proceeds as follows. Table~\ref{tab:rubric} maps the submission to the deliverable criteria. Sections~\ref{sec:level}--\ref{sec:testing} explain level design, narrative, systems, progression, and testing. Section~\ref{sec:technical} notes implementation and limits. Section~\ref{sec:upcoming} states planned work for Deliverable~3. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} closes the document.

\section{Level Design and Player Guidance}\label{sec:level}

Each district is laid out to shape how players move and how quickly danger rises, not only to look like a backdrop. The game guides the player clearly but still allows exploration. Besides tutorial text where needed, the design uses route structure, landmarks, mission prompts, and shifts in pace between calm exploration and night combat so the player knows where to go and what to do next.

Level~1 introduces the campaign. Its path passes major landmarks (crash site, military checkpoint, hospital) that read as distinct so players can orient and follow the mission without getting lost. Level~2 uses the same ideas but with more open ground and exposure, which raises tension and makes navigation, looting, and defense planning harder.

The two levels step up difficulty in a deliberate order. Level~1 teaches the day--night rhythm and preparation loop. Level~2 asks players to apply that knowledge with wider sight lines and tighter timing. Difficulty increases through layout and encounter spacing as well as through enemy strength.

Player guidance is delivered through several overlapping cues:

\begin{itemize}
    \item mission titles and live objective status through the in-game objective HUD,
    \item world-space objective markers that point toward the next important location,
    \item radio transmissions that give short narrative and tactical direction,
    \item recognizable landmarks that help the player build mental maps of each space,
    \item pacing shifts between day exploration and night defense.
\end{itemize}

Guidance and player freedom are balanced: optional loot and lore remain available, while several cues (listed below) repeat the main goal so it stays clear. The game aims for difficulty from decisions and time pressure, not from mystery about the objective. When players fail, it should be clear that preparation or positioning mattered.

\noindent\textbf{Summary of level choices.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Both levels use clear paths, readable landmark shapes, and mission locations so players can orient quickly during combat.
    \item Objective title, status text, world marker, and radio lines all point to the same goal through different channels.
    \item Level~2 adds more open ground, longer approaches, and less room to recover than Level~1.
    \item The critical path is suggested, not forced; players still choose loot order, how to engage, and where to fall back.
\end{itemize}

\section{Narrative Design and World-Building}\label{sec:narrative}

Narrative follows a play-first approach. Story arrives during movement, scavenging, defense, and mission completion rather than in long cutscenes. Story beats are spaced so they support play instead of stopping it, which keeps players who only follow main objectives on track while still rewarding those who read optional lore.

In the Deliverable 2 build, the player is presented as the survivor of a failed evacuation attempt. Across six nights, the player receives instructions and updates from EVAC Command while moving through two unsafe districts in search of supplies, answers, and a path to extraction. As the campaign progresses, the radio dialogue and mission structure reveal more about Project Lazarus, Dr. Chen, and Subject 23. This gives the run a stronger narrative arc than a simple wave-based survival mode.

Delivery uses several channels:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Radio lines state the situation, discoveries, and rising threat.
    \item Story objectives tie plot beats to places the player must reach.
    \item Optional lore (logs, orders, intercepts) adds depth for interested players.
    \item Environments are dressed to suggest districts that were held, then abandoned.
    \item Short intro and ending sequences frame the playable run.
\end{itemize}

The main plot stays clear for players who skip optional content; optional content still adds depth. Story, rewards, and mechanics all support the same survival premise so play does not fight the fiction.

\noindent\textbf{Summary of narrative choices.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Context is spread across missions so it appears before, during, and after combat spikes.
    \item EVAC Command, field voice, and intercept-style lines are kept distinct in tone and role.
    \item Objectives sit in spaces that fit the story (crash site, checkpoint, clinic or shelter chain).
    \item Lore never blocks required progress, so first-time players are not gated on collectibles.
\end{itemize}

\section{Systems Design and Game Balance}\label{sec:systems}

Systems tie daytime preparation to nighttime survival. The economy uses points, health, armor, ammunition, utility items, and weapon access. Resources stay scarce enough that tradeoffs matter, and the UI shows gains and spends clearly. The design keeps one question in focus: what to spend now versus what to save for later.

Daytime play supports four goals:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Gather ammo, healing, and utility for defense.
    \item Claim contested drops for higher rewards under time pressure.
    \item Finish mission leads for points, ammo, and story direction.
    \item Loot efficiently without ignoring the approaching night.
\end{itemize}

Strong daytime play yields better gear, healing, and position for night; weak daytime play forces riskier nights. The link from day to night is explicit so players can reason about cause and effect.

Balance depends on enemy tuning and on how clearly the game communicates threats and options before night begins. Rewards, missions, resources, and danger are wired together so planning is rewarded instead of guesswork.

\noindent\textbf{Summary of systems choices.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Pickup callouts and HUD counts show what changed after looting or shopping.
    \item Objective results, scavenging, and shop buys all feed into how survivable the night feels.
    \item Missed day objectives can strengthen the next night and reduce how many points carry forward; radio and summary UI explain this so the rule is visible, not hidden.
    \item Pickup flow emphasizes combat items (ammo, health, utility, points) to limit inventory clutter.
\end{itemize}

\section{Progression Systems and Rewards}\label{sec:progression}

Progression runs on two timescales. In the short term, each day--night--dawn cycle offers new resources, points, objectives, and shop choices. Over the full slice, six nights across two levels add unlocks, harder nights, and story movement from Town Center to Suburban.

Rewards mix skill growth with concrete loot. Players improve routing and judgment while also earning points, weapons, armor, mission payouts, contested bundles, and upgrades. Logs and environmental detail add story depth even when combat rewards are small.

Including Level~2 shows campaign continuity instead of a single map. After Level~1 is cleared, Level~2 unlocks and the player selects it from the menu. Each level starts from a clean state so difficulty stays comparable in testing.

\noindent\textbf{Summary of progression choices.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Level~2 requires Level~1 completion; the menu makes order explicit.
    \item The game does not auto-start the next level; the player chooses when to deploy.
    \item Each day--night step closes with spend-and-prepare feedback; clearing a level grants a larger milestone.
    \item Fresh starts per level avoid muddying balance with leftover stats from the previous map.
\end{itemize}

\section{Testing and Iteration}\label{sec:testing}

Iteration relied on watching what players did (hesitation, retries, questions) and on Play Mode checks in Unity. When players were confused, we treated it as a communication problem in the build, not only as subjective taste. Early tests showed the premise was clear but story and system links sometimes failed on first contact. We responded with clearer radio, mission text, HUD markers, pickup hints, reward messages, and dusk summaries so players could see what they had earned and why it mattered at night.

Further work focused on fair, readable rules: controls and systems must work, and players should understand them quickly enough to make informed choices. Updates targeted when and how the game reports changes so each feature reads clearly from the UI without outside explanation.

\noindent\textbf{Summary of testing practice.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Each pass targeted one concrete confusion (for example overlapping UI, unclear objectives, or busy comms).
    \item Most fixes stayed in HUD layout, triggers, and copy so we could re-test quickly without large rewrites.
    \item Day-to-night, night-to-dawn, level completion, and win/lose paths were rechecked after substantive changes.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Playtesting protocol.}
Sessions used a short, repeatable format:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Who:} classmates and informal testers with mixed experience (record the total session count for the final submission if the rubric requires it).
    \item \textbf{Task:} start a fresh Level~1 run; complete at least one full day--night--dawn loop; try a story objective; open the shop at dawn; stop when stuck or after twenty minutes.
    \item \textbf{Method:} think-aloud on the first loop when possible; note hesitation, mis-clicks, and misread HUD text without coaching mid-task.
    \item \textbf{Notes:} time to understand the objective, whether the world marker was seen, confusion after contested drops, and whether dusk summaries answered ``what did I earn?''
    \item \textbf{Changes:} one fix category per iteration (for example comms clarity versus marker overlap) before re-testing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Technical Notes}\label{sec:technical}

Several Unity systems supported this iteration:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Game flow and campaign state:} \texttt{GameManager} and \texttt{GameFlowController} coordinate the day, night, dawn, and level transition structure.
    \item \textbf{Mission guidance:} \texttt{StoryObjective}, the objective HUD, and objective markers provide in-world direction without relying on a heavy tutorial layer.
    \item \textbf{Narrative support:} radio updates, mission scripting, and lore systems support layered story delivery across the run.
    \item \textbf{Balance and preparation systems:} \texttt{SupplyCrate}, \texttt{Pickup}, \texttt{PointsSystem}, and the wave balancing path connect day preparation to night pressure.
    \item \textbf{Current feature gating:} crafting hooks exist in code but are disabled in this Deliverable 2 build (\texttt{enableCrafting = false}) to keep the prototype loop clean and readable.
    \item \textbf{Submission structure:} the Deliverable 2 package is organized around the Unity project, the standalone build output, and this written PDF report.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Build verification and limits.}
The team smoke-tested phase transitions and unlock flow in the Unity Editor (Play Mode) and in standalone builds on the main development platform. Crafting remains off (\texttt{enableCrafting = false}) to keep this milestone focused. Levels~3 and~4 appear as menu art only and are \textbf{not} playable in Deliverable~2.

\section{Upcoming Development (Deliverable 3)}\label{sec:upcoming}

Next work extends the Deliverable~2 baseline into a full four-level campaign:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Content:} make \textbf{Levels~3 and~4} (Industrial and Research) playable end-to-end; tune late-campaign waves and the \textbf{final encounter}.
    \item \textbf{Narrative:} add level-specific radio and objectives for Levels~3--4 so the Lazarus arc finishes coherently.
    \item \textbf{Presentation:} pass on HUD/UI clarity, environment readability, palette cohesion, and SFX/music where time allows.
    \item \textbf{Systems:} rebalance economy and progression across all four levels; consider re-enabling \textbf{crafting} only if it stays legible.
    \item \textbf{Stability:} fix blocking bugs, tighten phase transitions and unlock flow, and smoke-test the Windows build across the full route.
    \item \textbf{Player clarity:} clearer resume/restart messaging, stronger end-of-run summaries, and scoreboard polish for the longer campaign.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Deliverable~2 moves the project from a basic survival test to a more complete slice. The build shows clearer levels and guidance, narrative that works with play, systems that players can reason about, and progression across two linked maps instead of one.

Mechanics now match the setting and the target experience: players scavenge, follow missions, listen to radio updates, and see how daytime choices tie to night pressure and rewards. That integration is the main result of this milestone and sets up the larger campaign planned for Deliverable~3.
