\section{Project Overview}

\textit{Deadlight: Survival After Dark} is a top-down 2D zombie survival game built in Unity. The player controls a lone survivor moving through four environments (Town Center, Suburban, Industrial, and Research Facility), each containing three campaign nights, for a total of twelve nights across four levels.

Each session cycles through three phases. The \textbf{Day Phase} involves exploration, story objectives, and supply collection. The \textbf{Night Phase} involves defending against escalating enemy waves and responding to optional helicopter drops. The \textbf{Dawn Phase} is a shop screen where the player spends earned points on weapons, armour, and upgrades before the next night. A narrative arc involving Project Lazarus, EVAC Command, and a mutant subject called Subject~23 is delivered through radio messages, lore collectibles, and scripted story beats across all four levels.

\textbf{Deliverable 3 scope.} Deliverable 2 covered Levels 1 and 2. For Deliverable 3, the team completed Levels 3 and 4 with new maps and story content, added a three-phase boss fight against Subject~23, introduced a run modifier system for replay variation, implemented dynamic audio mixing, added a run-grading and leaderboard system, and performed a full playtesting and balance pass across all twelve nights.

\textbf{Submission contents.} The submission package contains: the Unity project folder (Assets, Packages, and ProjectSettings directories only), a Windows executable build (.exe and Data folder) verified to run without the Unity Editor, this PDF report, and a recorded showcase video (3--5 minutes) covering the game premise, a walkthrough of key systems, and the final boss encounter. For TA evaluation convenience, the submitted Deliverable~3 build keeps all four playable levels selectable from the level-deploy menu so testing can jump directly to any level without replaying the full campaign route each time.

\section{Art, Aesthetics \& Sound Design}

\subsection{Visual Direction and Colour Schemes}

Each map has its own colour palette so that the four levels are easy to tell apart at a glance. Town Center uses grey road tiles and beige building facades. Suburban adds green vegetation and lighter ground tones. Industrial uses a brown earth and dark warehouse palette with coloured storage units as landmarks. Research Facility uses a uniform mid-grey tile scheme with red-roofed institutional buildings at the centre, which produces a more enclosed, clinical look.

Accent colours are kept consistent across all maps and only used where they carry gameplay meaning: gold for objectives and supply drops, red for danger, and blue for interactive objects. A player can read colour as information without having to re-learn what it means on each new level.

\textbf{Procedural sprites.} \texttt{ProceduralSpriteGenerator} builds the character and environment sprites at runtime. Enemies share a base zombie shape, and \texttt{ZombieAnimator} handles the per-type colour tint and scale differences. Subject~23 scales up to 2x as the encounter starts, so it reads as different from standard enemies without needing a separate art asset. Supply crates are colour-coded by tier (Common brown, Rare blue, Legendary gold) so the player can tell value at a glance.

\textbf{Bullet trail.} Each bullet uses an 8-pixel sprite with a bright centre fading to orange at the edge, combined with a \texttt{TrailRenderer} set to fade over 55~ms. This makes shot direction and weapon fire rate readable during combat.

\textbf{Atmosphere.} \texttt{AtmosphereController} applies a colour tint over the scene that shifts from a warm tone during the day to a dark blue during the night, in sync with the day-night timer. \texttt{VFXManager} handles screen shake on explosions, heavy hits, and the boss entrance. \texttt{DecalManager} places blood marks on the ground that persist until the end of the night, so areas where combat occurred look different from untouched areas.

\subsection{User Interface}

The HUD is divided into five fixed areas on screen. These positions remain consistent across all levels and phases so the player does not need to re-learn the layout.

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Top-left:} Health bar, armour bar, active objective, and COMMS feed.
  \item \textbf{Top-centre:} Level name and wave number during the night, or phase label during the day.
  \item \textbf{Top-right:} Enemy count and, when a helicopter drop is scheduled, a countdown timer.
  \item \textbf{Bottom-right:} Weapon slots (four hotkeys), active weapon stats, throwable counts, and current ammo.
  \item \textbf{Bottom-left:} Score.
\end{itemize}

Shared fonts, colours, and panel sizes live in \texttt{UITheme}, which all runtime-built panels pull from. The Dawn shop is split into three tabs (Weapons, Upgrades, Armour) with a consumable row above them. The dark-background, coloured-button style is reused across the HUD, the Dawn shop, the Level Complete panel, and the Victory screen, so the interface feels like one thing and not four separate screens.

\subsection{Sound Design}

\texttt{AudioManager} handles five music tracks (menu, day, night, boss, dawn) and crossfades between them over 3.2 seconds on each state change. A tension value is also computed at runtime from the current phase: 0.05 during the day, 0.28 at night, and 0.80 during the boss fight. This value drives music volume, pitch, and ambient level, so the mix shifts with the game instead of cutting between fixed tracks.

A pool of ten \texttt{AudioSource} components handles spatial sound effects (gunfire, enemy sounds, explosions) with a 20-metre range. This lets the player hear threats off-screen before seeing them. \texttt{ProceduralAudioGenerator} produces pickup and interaction sounds at runtime. When a COMMS message plays, the music volume drops by about 24\% so the speech is audible, with stronger ducking for high-priority transmissions.

\section{Narrative \& Systems Integration}

\subsection{Story Arc}

The campaign follows a containment story across four levels. Each level advances a distinct part of the narrative.

\begin{tcolorbox}[tableframe]
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Level} & \textbf{Map} & \textbf{Story content} \\
\midrule
1 & Town Center & The evacuation has failed. The player makes first contact with EVAC Command and finds early signs of Project Lazarus at a crash site, checkpoint, and clinic. \\
2 & Suburban & Quarantine records are discovered. Lore drops reveal details of the Lazarus research programme. \\
3 & Industrial & A Lazarus laboratory is confirmed. Intercepted communications from Dr.\ Chen document the collapse of the supply chain. \\
4 & Research Facility & The player fights to contain Subject~23 in a three-phase boss encounter. EVAC extraction is triggered if Subject~23 is defeated. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Narrative Delivery}

A player who ignores the story can still finish every night. All of the narrative content is optional or ambient, and it reaches the player through these systems:

\textbf{Radio / COMMS.} \texttt{RadioTransmissions} queues messages from EVAC Command, Dr.~Chen, and survivor voices. A 1.2-second protection window in \texttt{NarrativeManager} prevents new messages from cutting off the one currently playing. Messages appear in a panel above the objective display with a channel label so the source is clear.

\textbf{Story Objectives.} \texttt{StoryObjective} assigns a title, description, and two-phase structure (Day Investigation, then Night Survival) to each of the twelve nights. Completed entries are stored in a list and displayed in \texttt{NarrativeJournalUI}, which the player can open at any time to review progress.

\textbf{Environmental lore.} \texttt{EnvironmentalLore} and \texttt{LorePickup} place optional items (graffiti, lab notes, survivor logs) across each map. These do not affect progression. The Level Complete screen reports a lore count as a secondary metric, such as ``Lore Found: 0/25.''

\textbf{Player voice.} \texttt{PlayerVoice} triggers short audio reactions tied to events (low health, first kill, boss spotted) using procedurally generated clips.

\subsection{Enemy AI Subsystems}

The game uses three enemy AI types that become more common in later levels.

\textbf{Standard enemies} (\texttt{EnemyAI}) use a direct pursuit behaviour with aggression-phase scaling. \texttt{EnemyAggressionPhase} increases enemy movement speed and attack frequency as the night progresses, so early waves in a night are easier than later ones within the same wave set. \texttt{EnemyAffix} attaches per-enemy modifiers (such as armoured, fast, or explosive death) that are activated by the run modifier system in higher-difficulty nights.

\textbf{Spitter enemies} (\texttt{SpitterAI}) keep their distance and fire projectiles at the player. They appear in Level 2 and become more common in Levels 3 and 4. They force the player to watch positioning instead of just kiting in a circle.

\textbf{Subject~23} (\texttt{BossController}) is the three-phase boss encounter described in Section~\ref{sec:boss}. On Level 4 Night 3 the player has to deal with standard enemies, ranged spitters, and the boss all at once. This is the only fight where all three of these AI systems are pushing on the player at the same time.

\subsection{Narrative Consequences in Gameplay}

Failing a story objective has mechanical effects. A missed \texttt{StoryObjective} adds a 1.2x enemy strength multiplier to the following night and increments a 0.75x carryover-penalty stack. Retry and forced-advance outcomes are announced through separate COMMS messages so the player understands the difference. Contested day drops follow the same pattern: EVAC Command announces the drop by radio, an objective marker shows the location, and the outcome is reported back through COMMS. Every supply event has a matching in-world message.

\section{Progression \& Balance}

\subsection{Campaign Structure}

The campaign has four levels, each containing three nights. Baseline progression data is still tracked in \texttt{PlayerPrefs} using two keys (\texttt{Deadlight\_UnlockedLevel} and \texttt{Deadlight\_HighestCompletedLevel}). For the submitted evaluation build, \texttt{GameManager} enables an all-levels-unlocked mode so any level can be selected directly from the deploy screen for TA testing. Each level still starts with a full reset of points, inventory, weapon loadout (pistol only), armour, throwables, and medkits. Within a level, the player builds up resources across three nights and spends them at the Dawn shop.

\subsection{Night Pacing}

Day and night durations are set individually for each of the twelve nights.

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{} l c c @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Level (nights)} & \textbf{Day duration (s)} & \textbf{Night duration (s)} \\
\midrule
Level 1 (N1--3) & 90, 80, 70 & 55, 65, 80 \\
Level 2 (N4--6) & 85, 75, 65 & 55, 70, 85 \\
Level 3 (N7--9) & 80, 70, 60 & 65, 80, 100 \\
Level 4 (N10--12) & 75, 65, 55 & 80, 100, 140 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Day phases shorten as the campaign moves forward, which cuts the amount of preparation time. Night phases get longer, which stretches the survival window. We deliberately kept the baseline enemy stats the same between levels and let the difficulty curve come from phase length and resource availability instead.

\subsection{Adaptive Resource Scaling}

Health and ammo pickup counts decrease slightly across levels and are further adjusted at runtime by the player's current reserve ammo.

\begin{tcolorbox}[tableframe]
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{} l c c c c @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Resource} & \textbf{Level 1} & \textbf{Level 2} & \textbf{Level 3} & \textbf{Level 4} \\
\midrule
Health pickups & 4 & 4 & 3 & 3 \\
Ammo pickups (base) & 5 & 5 & 4 & 4 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\vspace{6pt}
\textbf{Ammo pickup count by reserve ammo:} \\
Reserve $\geq 320$: no pickups. \quad Reserve $\geq 240$: 25\% of base. \\
Reserve $\geq 160$: 50\% of base. \quad Below 160: full count.
\end{tcolorbox}

The pickup spawner uses a simple scatter algorithm with up to 100 attempts per pickup. Each attempt biases toward the outer 40\% of the map, keeps at least 6 units of space between pickups, rejects anything within 10 units of the player's spawn point, and checks registered obstacle colliders so pickups do not end up inside buildings.

\subsection{Supply Crate Tiers and Helicopter Drops}

Crates use the same three-tier system as visual design: Common (brown), Rare (blue), Legendary (gold). Tier probability increases with night number.

\begin{align*}
P(\mathrm{Legendary}) &= \min(0.12,\ 0.01 + 0.01 \times \mathrm{night}) \\
P(\mathrm{Rare}) &= \min(0.35,\ 0.08 + 0.02 \times \mathrm{night})
\end{align*}

Night helicopter drops have a 65\% base spawn chance, reduced when the player's reserve ammo is high. The drop arrives after a 55-second delay with up to 8 seconds of random variation. Contested day drops trigger at 68\% through the day phase with a 60\% base chance.

\subsection{Run Modifier System}

\texttt{RunModifierSystem} selects one run modifier at run start from eight types (Fast Fragile, Horde Night, Blood Moon, Glass Cannon, and others). The active modifier changes enemy health, speed, and damage multipliers for the run. A separate nightly world-event roll (Thick Fog, Full Moon, Contamination, Reinforcements) is applied each night. Both are seeded from elapsed play time so runs vary without manually authored per-night scripts.

\subsection{Points Economy}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Night survived:} 100 base points plus a 50-point bonus per night survived.
  \item \textbf{Contested drop secured:} Common +90, Rare +140, Legendary +220 points plus loot.
  \item \textbf{Kill streak bonus:} tracked by \texttt{KillStreakSystem}.
  \item \textbf{Level transition:} the next level starts fresh with a full equipment reset.
\end{itemize}

\texttt{RunGradingSystem} produces a letter grade based on kills, accuracy, and points at the end of a run. The grade and a leaderboard rank from \texttt{LeaderboardManager} are shown on the victory screen.

\section{Playtesting \& Iteration}

\subsection{Feedback Sources and Protocol}

Between Deliverable~2 and Deliverable~3 we pulled feedback from three places:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Internal team sessions.} The four of us rotated through the build. Whoever was playing that day started a fresh run from Night~1 without being told what had just changed, and wrote anything odd into a shared iteration log.
  \item \textbf{Teaching assistant feedback.} The Deliverable~2 submission came back with written TA comments on onboarding clarity (see Section~\ref{sec:d2-peer-fb}). Those comments are what drove the interaction-prompt changes in Deliverable~3.
  \item \textbf{External tester session.} We ran one session with a peer who had never seen the game to watch first-contact reactions without coaching. The tester was asked to think out loud, and one of us sat out of view taking notes.
\end{itemize}

Each session followed the same protocol:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Start a fresh run from Night~1 without advance knowledge of recent changes.
  \item Play through at least one complete day, night, and dawn cycle, and where possible a full level.
  \item Record observations on objective clarity, resource availability, COMMS timing, combat feel, and any moment where the player was unsure what to do.
  \item Address one category of issues before the next session to avoid interference between fixes.
\end{enumerate}

The \texttt{Assets/Tests/PlayMode} folder holds a PlayMode regression suite with three test files: \texttt{EdgeFlowSmokeRuntimeTests} (retry, forced-advance, and penalty carry-over), \texttt{WeaponLoadoutRuntimeTests} (weapon slots and ammo reserves), and \texttt{LevelTransitionResetRuntimeTests} (level-transition state reset). We ran the full suite before submission and everything passed, so the changes above did not break anything that was already working.

\subsection{Teaching Assistant Feedback on Deliverable 2}
\label{sec:d2-peer-fb}

The written feedback on the Deliverable~2 build was positive on overall feel but identified two concrete onboarding gaps:

\begin{tcolorbox}[tableframe]
\small
\textit{``One thing that can be improved is when press F to loot object, player needs to have a long press. But this is not instructed clearly. As well as press space to accelerate.''}
\end{tcolorbox}

Both points were fair: the loot interaction is a hold, not a tap, and Space triggers a dodge roll, not an ``accelerate.'' Both were communication problems, not mechanical ones. The response in Deliverable~3 was:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Hold-to-loot clarity (\texttt{SupplyCrate.cs}).} The progress bar now becomes visible as soon as the player enters loot range, rather than only appearing once F is pressed. An empty bar sitting next to the prompt makes the hold requirement self-evident: tapping F no longer looks like it did nothing. The prompt text reads \texttt{Hold F to Loot} or \texttt{Hold F to Secure} for contested drops.
  \item \textbf{Movement-control hint card (\texttt{FirstCombatHintController.cs}).} At the start of Night~1, before enemies appear, the HUD displays a transient status line reading \textit{WASD: Move \;--\; Shift: Sprint \;--\; Space: Dodge Roll}, followed by \textit{Walk up to a crate and hold F to loot it}. This introduces the two controls flagged in the TA feedback (Space and F) in the player's own vocabulary, before combat begins.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Changes from Deliverable 2 to Deliverable 3}

The table below lists the main mechanic and balance changes we made in response to specific problems we saw during playtesting. Boss and performance changes are covered in their own subsections.

\begin{tcolorbox}[tableframe]
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} p{3.5cm} X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Observed problem} & \textbf{Change made} \\
\midrule
TA feedback: hold-to-loot not communicated & Progress bar now appears on proximity, not on key press, so the hold requirement is visible before the player acts (Section~\ref{sec:d2-peer-fb}). \\
\addlinespace
TA feedback: Space mistaken for ``accelerate'' & Movement-control status card shown on Night~1 start labels Space as \textit{Dodge Roll} and Shift as \textit{Sprint}. \\
\addlinespace

\addlinespace

\addlinespace

\addlinespace

\addlinespace

\addlinespace

\addlinespace

\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Boss Encounter Iteration: Fairness, Tension, Accomplishment}

The Subject~23 encounter went through more iteration than any other part of the game. The first playable version was a single stat block with high health and contact damage. Testers flagged two problems: the fight felt random on first contact (one-shot deaths with no warning), and once the player worked out to kite the boss in circles the tension vanished. The encounter was failing both on \textit{fairness} and on \textit{tension}, and winning did not feel earned because the outcome felt arbitrary.

The team reworked the fight in three passes. Each pass came from a specific playtest observation.

\begin{tcolorbox}[tableframe]
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} p{2.3cm} p{4cm} X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Pass} & \textbf{Observation} & \textbf{Design change} \\
\midrule
Pass 1 (fairness) & Players were killed without understanding what hit them. & The encounter was split into three phases tied to boss health, with clear visual and audio cues on transitions. Phase 1 (>60\% HP) is pure pursuit, which teaches the boss's movement before any new attack is added. \\
\addlinespace
Pass 2 (tension) & Once pursuit was learned, circle-strafing defeated the boss with no risk. & Phase 2 (60--30\% HP) adds charge attacks at 12 units per second that punish kiting a straight line. The player has to break line of sight or dodge-roll to survive. \\
\addlinespace
Pass 3 (accomplishment) & The final stretch felt anticlimactic once the boss was low. & Phase 3 (<30\% HP) adds a seismic slam with a 4-unit radius and 30 area damage. This is the boss's loudest move, and it forces one more behaviour shift right before defeat so the last 30\% of the fight is the hardest. Standard enemies and spitters continue to spawn, so the player is managing three threat types at once when the boss finally goes down. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

A few supporting changes made the outcome feel earned. A red screen flash and a 0.8-second screen shake on boss spawn signal a clear state change, the music switches to the boss track over a crossfade instead of cutting, and the victory state is gated on \texttt{WaveManager.\-IsFinalBossDefeatedForCurrentNight()} so the night cannot end while the boss is still alive. A ``Final approach bonus'' milestone awarded by \texttt{ProgressionManager} after Night~11 adds points to the Dawn shop before Night~12, so the player can top up ammo and armour before the fight even after a rough Night~11. In the final round of playtests, the fight killed players on the first attempt but was winnable on the second or third, which is the fairness-and-accomplishment curve the team was aiming for.

\subsection{Debugging and Performance Optimizations}

A handful of stability and responsiveness issues came up during testing. They were all fixed before submission:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Duplicate player on scene reload.} Returning to the main menu and starting a new run occasionally produced two \texttt{PlayerController} instances in the scene (one from the persistent managers, one from the new scene). \texttt{GameManager.EnsurePlayerExists()} now checks for multiple instances on load and disables any extras before gameplay resumes.
  \item \textbf{Physics accumulation on unpause.} The first frame after unpausing previously showed a velocity spike because \texttt{Time.deltaTime} had accumulated while paused. \texttt{Time.fixedDeltaTime} is now clamped on pause and the accumulated delta is discarded on unpause.
  \item \textbf{Audio allocation spikes during Night 12.} The boss wave triggers many overlapping SFX. A pool of ten pre-allocated \texttt{AudioSource} components now handles spatial sound, which removed the per-sound instantiation cost and eliminated the audible stutter observed during stress testing.
  \item \textbf{Pickup placement stalls.} The original 100-attempt scatter loop iterated to the maximum every frame when the map was full. It now exits on the first valid position, and falls back to a corner-quadrant placement if no valid spot is found, so placement never fails silently and never stalls the frame.
  \item \textbf{Level transition hitches.} Procedural map rebuild between levels was originally a coroutine, which produced visible flickers as buildings spawned over several frames. It is now a single-frame synchronous rebuild inside \texttt{TestSceneSetup.RebuildMap()}, and manager state is preserved because the scene itself is not reloaded.
  \item \textbf{COMMS speech masked by music.} Testers reported missing EVAC messages during loud combat. Music volume is now ducked by approximately 24\% while a COMMS line plays, with stronger ducking for high-priority transmissions.
  \item \textbf{Night-start warning easy to miss.} The 15-second warning before night was audio-only. A low-amplitude screen shake now plays with it, giving a second signal channel for players focused on looting.
\end{itemize}

We also re-ran the PlayMode suite after each of the changes listed above to catch regressions early, and it stayed green the whole time.

\section{Technical Implementation}

\subsection{Build and Project Structure}

The submission includes the Unity project folder with the three required directories: Assets, Packages, and ProjectSettings. The Library and Temp directories are excluded. A Windows build is included in the Builds/Windows folder. The build was verified to launch and run through a full campaign night without the Unity Editor present, with no missing asset errors or critical crashes.

Key performance measures implemented in the build:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Audio source pooling (10 pre-allocated sources) avoids per-sound object allocation during high-activity nights.
  \item Bullet objects are created and destroyed normally; the small per-frame count does not require a full pool, and this was confirmed acceptable during stress testing on Night 12.
  \item Pickup placement uses early-exit collision checks so the 100-attempt scatter loop exits as soon as a valid position is found rather than always iterating to the maximum.
  \item \texttt{Time.fixedDeltaTime} is clamped when pausing to prevent physics accumulation on unpause.
  \item The procedural map rebuild (level transition) runs in a single frame with synchronous instantiation instead of a coroutine, so the transition does not visibly stutter.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Architecture}

The project uses a set of singleton manager objects that communicate through C\# events. All managers persist across scene loads via \texttt{DontDestroyOnLoad}. \texttt{GameManager} uses \texttt{[RuntimeInitializeOnLoadMethod]} to create any missing managers at startup, so the build functions correctly even if a manager prefab is absent from the scene.

\subsection{Game State Machine}

\texttt{GameManager.GameState} defines seven states covering the full session flow.

\begin{center}
\small
\texttt{MainMenu} $\to$ \texttt{DayPhase} $\to$ \texttt{NightPhase} $\to$ \texttt{DawnPhase} \\[2pt]
$\to$ \texttt{LevelComplete} $\to$ \texttt{Victory} \,/\, \texttt{GameOver}
\end{center}

Each state change fires the \texttt{OnGameStateChanged} C\# event. \texttt{AudioManager}, \texttt{AtmosphereController}, UI panels, and \texttt{GameFlowController} each subscribe independently. Adding a new system that needs to respond to state changes does not require editing \texttt{GameManager}.

\subsection{Procedural Map System}

Each level has a dedicated builder class (\texttt{TownCenterBuilder}, \texttt{SuburbanBuilder}, \texttt{IndustrialBuilder}, \texttt{ResearchBuilder}) extending \texttt{MapBuilderBase}. Each builder places buildings, roads, obstacles, spawn points, and lore props according to map-specific layout and landmark rules. When the player advances to the next level, \texttt{TestSceneSetup.RebuildMap()} removes the previous map and constructs the new one in the same scene without a scene load, so all manager state is preserved across the transition.

\subsection{Boss Fight}
\label{sec:boss}

\texttt{BossController} runs the Subject~23 encounter across three phases.

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Phase 1} (above 60\% health): The boss pursues the player using \texttt{SimpleEnemyAI}.
  \item \textbf{Phase 2} (30--60\% health): The boss adds charge attacks at 12 units per second with 40 damage on contact.
  \item \textbf{Phase 3} (below 30\% health): The boss adds a seismic slam with a 4-unit radius and 30 area damage.
\end{itemize}

When the boss spawns, a coroutine plays a red screen flash, a 0.8-second screen shake, and switches the music to the boss track. The night does not end until \texttt{WaveManager.\-IsFinalBossDefeatedForCurrentNight()} returns true, which prevents the victory state from triggering while the boss is still alive.

\subsection{Code Patterns}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Observer pattern:} C\# events are used throughout (\texttt{OnGameStateChanged}, \texttt{OnNight\-Changed}, \texttt{OnContestedDropState\-Changed}, \texttt{OnDayTimerUpdate}, \texttt{OnDawnPhaseStarted}, and others).
  \item \textbf{Object pooling:} Ten pre-allocated \texttt{AudioSource} components handle spatial SFX to avoid per-sound allocation.
  \item \textbf{Null safety and fallbacks:} All managers check for null before acting. The pickup placement function retries up to 100 times and falls back to a corner-quadrant position if no valid spot is found, so placement never fails silently.
  \item \textbf{Singleton scope:} \texttt{DontDestroyOnLoad} is applied only to managers. All gameplay objects are created and destroyed normally during play, keeping memory usage predictable across level transitions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

\textit{Deadlight: Survival After Dark} is a complete four-level survival game built around the design principles covered in the course. The MDA framework fits the finished project: the mechanics are the wave and economy loop, the dynamics are the risk-and-reward decisions the player makes during day and night, and the intended aesthetics are tension, exploration, and narrative engagement. Adaptive resource scaling, optional lore, spatial audio, and a radio-based story arc let the game support players with different priorities without forcing a single playstyle.

The main additions in Deliverable~3 over Deliverable~2 are Levels 3 and 4 with their maps, story beats, and enemy configurations, the three-phase boss fight, the resource balance changes driven by playtest sessions, and the audio and visual polish pass. Every change in this deliverable came from a specific problem observed during testing rather than a speculative design tweak.

The Windows build runs without the Unity Editor, and we played through a full twelve-night campaign on it before submitting to confirm there were no critical errors. Every system described in this report is active in that build. The showcase video walks through the game premise, the day--night--dawn loop, the contested drop system, and the boss encounter.
